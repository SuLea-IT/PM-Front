ABSTRACT
Plant de novo regeneration, unlike regeneration in most animal systems, relies on callus formation and re-establishment of stem-cell niches (SCNs) rather than on pre-existing reservoirs. Growth hormones such as auxin and cytokinin, together with numerous genetic regulators, participate in regeneration, but the architectural principles coordinating extensive cellular reprogramming at the individual-cell scale remain unknown. Using super-resolution multimodal spatial transcriptomics enabled by enhanced tissue fixation applied to ~1.2 million cells, we tracked tomato regeneration across temporal intervals from wounding to organogenesis. The process leads to the establishment of a previously unrecognized ovoid-structured stem-cell niche, composed of a signaling layer, a plastic compartment, and a quiescent core, reminiscent of animal regenerative niches, and depends on EPFL8b-mediated ligand–receptor signaling to coordinate tissue patterning with metabolic-state sensing for proper regeneration. These findings expand traditional gene-centric paradigms, highlighting structural organization as a fundamental principle in regeneration, and serve as a comprehensive resource for hypothesis-led functional characterization. Datasets and protocols are available at http://www.single-cell-spatial.com.
Key words: Plant regeneration, Spatial transcriptomics, Super-resolution, Stem-cell niche, EPFL8b signaling

INTRODUCTION
Regeneration, the capacity to replace damaged tissues or organs, serves as a fundamental pillar of biological resilience with striking evolutionary parallels across kingdoms (Clevers and Watt, 2018; Sánchez Alvarado and Yamanaka, 2014). Though plants and animals use divergent cellular strategies, their regenerative processes appear governed by deeply conserved principles of cellular plasticity (Sablowski, 2011; Sugimoto et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2017). Animal regeneration frequently depends on pre-existing stem-cell niches (SCNs), yet dysregulation of these programs risks oncogenesis. Plants, conversely, show extraordinary regenerative potential by reprogramming somatic cells into de novo SCNs, capable of regenerating entire organisms while remaining tightly constrained to prevent aberrant growth (Birnbaum and Alvarado, 2008; Dinneny and Benfey, 2008; Heidstra and Sabatini, 2014; Scheres, 2007). This precise control establishes plants as powerful models for interrogating universal principles of cellular plasticity and tissue patterning.
Studies on plant regeneration have centered on callus formation, a pluripotent cell mass triggered by wounding or exogenous hormone application (Chlyah, 1974; Skoog and Miller, 1957). Core roles are established for growth hormones (e.g., auxin and cytokinin) and regulators such as Wuschel (WUS), Regeneration Factor 1 (REF1), Baby Boom (BBM), the Clavata pathway (CLV1/CLV3), and Somatic Embryogenesis Receptor Kinases (SERK1/2) (Boutilier et al., 2002; Fletcher et al., 1999; Hecht et al., 2001; Laux et al., 1996; Skoog and Miller, 1957; Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2017), but persistent cellular heterogeneity in callus has obscured our understanding of how spatial dynamics of positional cues and cell-fate patterns contribute to plant regeneration (Eshed Williams, 2021; Ikeuchi et al., 2019). Single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq), single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and spatial transcriptomics illuminate this complexity (Giacomello et al., 2017; Moreno-Villena et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024; Zhai and Xu, 2021; Zhang et al., 2025), however, compared with animal systems, plant models still lag in resolving SCN organization due to technical limitations in spatial resolution (Farah et al., 2024; Greenwald et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Rhaman et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
Here, we resolve this disparity through a super-resolution spatial atlas of plant regeneration, integrating 1.16 million single-cell profiles from 101 spatiotemporally resolved transcriptomic maps obtained following tomato wounding. This approach deciphered the known tripartite callus architecture governed by graded pluripotency states (Chlyah, 1974; Zhai and Xu, 2021) and enabled discoveries, including the definition of a previously unrecognized ovoid-structured SCN extending beyond classical morphological descriptions (Sugimoto et al., 2010; Varapparambath et al., 2022), and resolved here with high spatial and conceptual resolution. As a Resource, this atlas establishes a comprehensive, subcellular-resolution reference of plant regeneration and a standardized workflow, serving as a benchmark for spatial transcriptomics in plants, with potential relevance to other systems.

RESULTS
Multiscale spatiotemporal mapping of shoot regeneration after wounding
To investigate the spatiotemporal regulation of shoot regeneration, we conducted a multiscale analysis across tomato cotyledon explants from 0–16 days post-excision (DPE), a well-established de novo regeneration system used for genetic transformation, and noted for reproducible callus induction and characteristic morphology (Supplemental Figure 1) (Skoog and Miller, 1957; Song et al., 2023). Histological frozen sections (including transverse and longitudinal sections, Supplemental Figure 2A) captured distinct developmental phases: stem-cell establishment (0–8 DPE, with 0 DPE immediately after excision) and SCN formation (8–16 DPE), with spatial transcriptomics applied at 2 DPE intervals for most time points (Figure 1A–1C). We integrated sequencing-based spatial transcriptomics (using BGI stereo-seq V1, BMK S1000/S3000) to construct transcriptional landscapes (Song et al., 2023), generating 60 spatial transcriptomes with a median detection of over 1,200 genes per cell at 5-μm resolution (Supplemental Figure 2B, Supplemental Data 1). Cell-type annotations were assigned by combining histological features with canonical marker-gene expression, resolving nine cell populations: upper epidermal cells (UECs), lower epidermal cells (LECs), stem-cell niches (SCNs), hypoxic niche cells (HNCs), higher-chlorenchyma cells (HCCs), lower-chlorenchyma cells (LCCs), phloem cells (PHCs), cambium cells (CACs), and xylem cells (XYCs). Complementary snRNA-seq (10x Chromium, BMK DG1000) and bulk RNA-seq analyses showed consistent results across technologies (Figure 1C, Supplemental Figure 2B–2D, Supplemental Data 2). 
To complement sequencing-based spatial transcriptomics, we incorporated imaging-based spatial transcriptomics (10x Xenium) targeting 50 key genes (related to hormone signaling, cell-type identity, developmental regulation, and cell-wall composition) selected from the sequencing-based data. Using targeted probe hybridization, ligation, and rolling-circle amplification to generate amplified barcode signals for selected transcripts, Xenium facilitated clearer visualization of key markers when transcript signals were sparse in the sequencing-based data (Figure 1A, Supplemental Data 3). This expanded the total number of profiled cells in our atlas to 1.16 million. Overall, sequencing-derived gene lists informed probe selection for imaging tools, integrating genome-wide expression data with accurate cellular localization to construct a multimodal spatiotemporal atlas of plant regeneration. To facilitate community engagement, we developed the Single Cell Spatial Platform (http://www.single-cell-spatial.com), providing open access to datasets, protocols, thereby serving as a durable community resource for plant spatial transcriptomics (Supplemental Figure 3B–3G).
Our integrated spatial transcriptomics enabled mesoscale-resolution (resolution between subcellular- and tissue-level imaging) mapping of gene-expression during plant regeneration. At low magnification (1×), nine major cell types were distinguishable (Figure 2A). At 10× and 100× magnifications, the BMK S3000 and BGI Stereo-seq panels showed locally concentrated signals of detected transcript signals in undifferentiated regions such as the SCNs, reflected by higher UMI counts per spot or MID counts per bin than in the surrounding tissues (Figure 2A). Subcellular imaging at 750× resolution visualized mRNA distribution within nuclear and peripheral cytoplasm compartments, with vacuolated regions exhibiting sparse signals (Figure 2A). At the single-cell level, spatial mapping highlighted dynamic cellular organization: MERISTEM LAYER 1 (ML1) primarily marked epidermal cells but sporadically appeared in internal callus regions, reflecting irregular expression heterogeneity (Figure 2B) (Lu et al., 1996; Sessions et al., 1999). Simultaneous spatial detection of marker genes distinguished epidermal and vascular cell types based on their localized transcript distributions. Epidermal cell marker gene is ML1, whereas the vascular cell marker genes include PHLOEM INTERCALATED WITH XYLEM (PXY) (Fisher and Turner, 2007), ATP-BINDING CASSETTE G14 (ABCG14) (Ko et al., 2014), and MONOPTEROS (MP/ARF5) (Hardtke and Berleth, 1998) (Figure 2C). The SCN organization became progressively clearer as increasing numbers of genes were overlaid in the merged Xenium images. In this descriptive visualization, overlaying WUS and SHOOT MERISTEMLESS (STM) first distinguished central and surrounding regions. Adding CLV1, CLV3, and PLETHORA 3a (PLT3a) revealed an ovoid-structured SCN, and the fully merged 10-gene panel further made the outer, inner, and core organization evident. In this structure, the outer region was enriched for CUP-SHAPED COTYLEDON 2 (CUC2) transcripts, the inner region was enriched for STM, CLV1, and PLT3a transcripts, and the core region was enriched for WUS and CLV3 transcripts. These domains were inferred from how the composite spatial pattern changed as additional genes were incrementally overlaid from 2 genes to 10 genes (Figure 2D). Time-course analysis captured gene-expression dynamics, such as WOUND INDUCED DEDIFFERENTIATION 1 (WIND1) (Figure 2E) (Yang et al., 2024). At the subcellular level, analysis uncovered compartment-specific gene activity during regeneration. DICER-LIKE 2d (DCL2d), involved in siRNA biogenesis (Bologna and Voinnet, 2014), localized predominantly to nuclei, whereas PROTEASE INHIBITOR 2 (PI-2) and Related to APETALA 2.6-like (RAP2.6L) (Ye et al., 2020) showed broadly distributed signals across both nuclear and cytoplasmic regions (Figure 2F). G2/M-phase cell-cycle regulators such as CYCLIN B1;2 (CYCB1;2) (Ito et al., 1998) and MYB DOMAIN PROTEIN 3R4 (MYB3R-4) (Haga et al., 2007) were enriched in nuclei with condensed chromosomes, contrasting with their sparse distribution in neighboring cells (Figure 2G) (Yang et al., 2021).
High-resolution Xenium analysis further subdivided the SCN into three sublayers by clustering Xenium-segmented SCN cells using the full 50-gene panel. CLV3, PLT3a, and CUC2 are representative marker genes enriched in the core, inner, and shell sublayers, respectively  (Figure 2H, Supplemental Figure 3H). This comprehensive atlas, leveraging super-resolution spatial multi-omics, reveals spatially organized cellular architecture underlying plant regeneration, uncovering a greater degree of cellular heterogeneity whose function remains to be elucidated. We hypothesize that the orderly and programmatic establishment of these structures plays a critical role in regeneration success and capacity, which we explore next.
In summary, this spatiotemporal atlas establishes a fully spatial, million-cell reference of plant regeneration, spanning tissue-, cellular-, and subcellular-resolutions. Beyond technical advances, the atlas delivers broadly generalizable insights, defining conserved callus zones, resolving SCN stratification into distinct sublayers, and uncovering compartment-specific gene activities. Together, these results deliver a high-resolution reference of regenerative architecture and offer a versatile, resource for benchmarking spatial technologies, facilitating cross-species comparisons and supporting hypothesis generation in plant developmental and regenerative biology.
